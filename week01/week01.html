As We May Think by Vannevar Bush, 1945

In the essay, “As We May Thing”, Vannevar Bush urges scientists to explore a new path to store knowledge. He argues that back then, scientists have been using their knowledge for war work. But now it is time for scientists to take a step forward and use their knowledge to extend “the power of his mind” rather than the “man’s physical power”. Bush argues that extending “the power of his mind" is crucial because it makes our storing of knowledge more accessible. Developing instruments properly will give people access to ages of knowledge. Currently, many modern powerful instruments have been invented for such purpose. The list of instruments includes photography, cathode ray tubes and many more. However, Bush urges that storing the knowledge is not enough. All the information collected should also be consulted. Overall, Bush’s excerpt lay the foundation for scientists to pursue a new direction: the web. However, when the excerpt was published in 1945, the web has not been developed yet. So, it was really intriguing to know how Bush has predicted how powerful and useful the web could be.

Long Live the Web by Tim Berners Lee, 2010

Tim Berners Lee developed the web in 1989. In “Long live the Web”, Lee informs us that it is essential to follow the principles of the web. As the developer of the web, he reminds us that the web is a public resource for everyone used to serve humanity. However, it has been threatened by totalitarian governments to invade the privacy of others and used for other negative purposes. To stop the web from being threatened, it makes the many principles of the web to be more valuable. The main principle of the web is universality. This means anyone should be able to access the web from anywhere and anything can be put on the web. This brings up a question: how does censorship challenge this principle? The other principal Lee brings up is decentralization. It is the freedom to make any page you want without asking for permission. The other principle is net neutrality. Lee urges us all to uphold the principles of the web so that we keep the web going and it becomes a tool for future innovation.

Some thoughts: After reading and analyzing both excerpts, both Bush and Lee indicate that the web is necessary to pave the path for the next generation of innovation. However, if Lee states that the web is being threatened, who is at fault since the web “belongs to everyone”. Also the principles that the Lee mentioned in the excerpt about universality. If anything can be posted on the web, can’t the web be also a dangerous platform? What are the limitations to the “anything” that can be posted?
